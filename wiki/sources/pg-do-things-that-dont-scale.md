# Do Things That Don't Scale — Paul Graham

**Source:** paulgraham.com/ds.html (July 2013)
**Type:** Essay — tactical doctrine for early-stage startups

---

## Core Thesis

Startups don't just take off. Founders make them take off. There is always a **separate, laborious process** to get them going — like a hand crank before the engine catches. The single most common unscalable thing founders must do: **manually recruit users.**

---

## The Unscalable Tactics

### 1. Recruit Users Manually
Don't wait for users to come to you. Go get them. Stripe's founders ("Collison installation") didn't say "we'll send you a link." They said "give me your laptop" and set people up on the spot.

Compound growth makes the early small numbers misleading. 100 users → 10% weekly growth → 14,000 users in a year → 2 million in two years. Getting 10 more users this week matters enormously.

### 2. Delight Early Users Obsessively
Wufoo sent handwritten thank-you notes to every new user for as long as they could. First users should feel signing up with you was one of the best choices they ever made.

The product doesn't need to be great. The **experience** of being your user needs to be great. "Insanely great" for a larval startup means over-engagement and obsessive attention, not perfect code.

### 3. The Consultant Trick (for B2B)
Pick one user. Act as if you're a consultant hired only for them. Fit their needs perfectly. The thing you build for one user will almost always be what many users want.

### 4. The Fire Strategy (Narrow Market)
Deliberately contain yourself to a subset of the market first. Get critical mass there before expanding. Facebook was Harvard-only → specific colleges → everyone. The narrowness is the feature, not the bug.

### 5. Be Your Own Software
When only a few users exist, you can manually do things that will later be automated. Stripe manually signed up first users for merchant accounts behind the scenes. This lets you launch faster AND learn exactly what to build.

---

## What Doesn't Work

- **Big Launch:** Nobody remembers startup launches except famous flops (Segway, Google Wave). You only need enough initial users to start a feedback loop. A few months later, what matters is how happy those users are, not how many.
- **Big Partners:** "That was way more work than we expected, and we ended up getting practically nothing out of it." Standard outcome. Don't rely on partnerships for early growth.

---

## Treat the Startup as a Vector, Not a Scalar

A startup idea = **(what you build) + (the unscalable thing you'll do to get it started).** If you can't name the unscalable thing — if there's no way to manually recruit first users — the idea is probably bad for you specifically.

---

## Key Quotes

> "Startups take off because the founders make them take off."
> "I have never once seen a startup lured down a blind alley by trying too hard to make their initial users happy."
> "It's not enough just to do something extraordinary initially. You have to make an extraordinary *effort* initially."
