# How to Get Startup Ideas — Paul Graham

**Source:** paulgraham.com/startupideas.html (November 2012)
**Type:** Essay — foundational PG doctrine on idea generation

---

## Core Thesis

Don't try to think of startup ideas. Look for problems — preferably problems you have yourself. The verb is **"notice,"** not "think up."

> "Live in the future, then build what's missing."
> (Better version: "Live in the future and build what seems interesting.")

---

## The Three Criteria for a Great Idea

A great startup idea is:
1. Something the founders themselves **want**
2. That they can **build**
3. That **few others realize** is worth doing

Microsoft, Apple, Yahoo, Google, and Facebook all started this way.

---

## The Well vs. The Pond

Most bad ideas look like shallow ponds: many people mildly interested ("social network for pet owners"). Almost all good startup ideas look like deep wells: **small group of people who urgently need it.**

Key test: **"Who wants this right now? Who wants it so much they'll use a crappy v1 from an unknown startup?"** If you can't answer that with a specific person, the idea is probably bad.

The well can expand into a pond — Microsoft started with Altair BASIC for a few thousand hobbyists. Facebook started Harvard-only. The initial narrowness is fine; what matters is a **fast path out** of the niche.

---

## Why Bad Ideas Feel Good (The "Sitcom" Problem)

Made-up ideas yield bad ideas that **sound plausible enough to fool you.** The response to test them is "yeah, maybe I could see using that" — not "I need this now." Sum "maybe" across a population = zero users.

The set of plausible-sounding ideas is many times larger than the set of good ones. Plausibility alone means nothing.

---

## The Organic Method

Good ideas come when **external stimuli hit a prepared mind.** Bill Gates heard about the Altair and saw it immediately because his mind was already in that space. Drew Houston forgot his USB stick and saw Dropbox because he was already living that problem.

**"If you're the right sort of person, you have the right sort of hunches."** Being at the leading edge of a rapidly changing field is what makes you the right sort of person. You don't need to push the field — you can be at the edge as a user.

---

## The Two Filters to Turn Off

**Schlep filter:** You unconsciously avoid ideas that involve tedious or painful work. Stripe. Thousands of programmers saw the payments pain. They looked away. Stripe didn't. This is the most dangerous filter because it's often illusory.

**Unsexy filter:** You avoid problems you despise. Less dangerous than schlep, but still kills opportunities. The best recipe: actively **seek out ideas that are unsexy or involve schleps.** They're waiting in the open because everyone else skipped them.

---

## Noticing Tricks

- Pay attention to what **chafes you** — annoyance is a signal that you're living in the future
- Work on things that seem **cool or like toys** — toys often turn out to matter more than expected (microcomputers, BackRub, Facebook)
- Ask: "Why doesn't someone make X? If they did, we'd buy it immediately"
- Look for dying industries and ask what would replace them (from the side, not head-on)
- Look at "scruffy sophisticated users" being ignored by big players — the early microcomputer hobbyist archetype
- Ask what **waves** are happening and how to ride them

---

## Competition Is Not the Problem

Feeling like you're late is a **sign of a good idea**, not a bad one. Startups are rarely killed by competitors. You need a thesis about what everyone else is overlooking — not just "we'll make one that doesn't suck."

A crowded market = demand exists + no solution is good enough. That's exactly what you want.

---

## Key Quotes

> "The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas."
> "Nearly all good startup ideas are of the deep-well type."
> "The most successful startups almost all begin as organic ideas."
> "Work on hard problems, driven mainly by curiosity, but have a second self watching over your shoulder, taking note of gaps and anomalies."
