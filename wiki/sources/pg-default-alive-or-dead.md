# Default Alive or Default Dead? — Paul Graham

**Source:** paulgraham.com/aord.html (October 2015)
**Type:** Essay — survival doctrine for operating startups

---

## Core Thesis

For any startup operating 8–9+ months: **if expenses stay constant and revenue growth continues at its recent rate, do you reach profitability on remaining cash?** This is the single most important question, and most founders don't know the answer.

- **Default Alive:** You make it to profitability on current trajectory. You can think ambitiously.
- **Default Dead:** Your trajectory ends in death. Every conversation should be about how to get off that trajectory.

---

## The Fatal Pinch

**Default dead + slow growth + not enough time to fix it.** Founders end up here by not realizing that's where they're headed.

The solution: **start asking this question too early, not too late.** The polarity switch — from "meaningless" to "critical" — happens gradually and catches founders off guard.

---

## Why Founders Get This Wrong

1. They assume fundraising is easy. It isn't. And the more you depend on it, the falser that assumption becomes.
2. VCs push founders to hire fast because kill-or-cure is optimal for a portfolio. It is not optimal for you.
3. Slow growth gets blamed on not having enough people. Almost never true. Slow growth is usually a product problem.

---

## How Startups Die (The Pattern)

1. Build something moderately appealing
2. Get decent initial growth
3. Raise a round easily (founders seem smart, idea sounds plausible)
4. Growth is ok but not great (product is only moderately appealing)
5. Founders decide to hire to boost growth. Investors agree.
6. Growth doesn't come. Now burning fast with no traction.
7. Unappealing to new investors. Can't raise. Company dies.

The company should have addressed the fundamental problem: **the product is only moderately appealing.** Hiring doesn't fix that. More often it makes it harder.

---

## The Rules

- **Don't hire too fast.** Overhiring is the biggest killer of funded startups.
- **Don't treat fundraising as Plan A without a written Plan B.** Know exactly what you'll do if you can't raise, and when you'll switch.
- **Slow growth → fix the product, not the headcount.** At early stage, the product needs to evolve, not be "built out." Fewer people makes that easier.
- **Growing fast vs. spending a lot are not correlated.** Fast growth comes from product-market fit, not from spend.

Airbnb waited 4 months after their YC raise before hiring their first employee. The founders were terribly overworked. But they were overworked evolving Airbnb into what it became.

---

## Key Quote

> "Hiring too fast is by far the biggest killer of startups that raise money."
> "When a startup grows fast, it's usually because the product hits a nerve... When a startup spends a lot, it's usually because the product is expensive to develop or sell, or simply because they're wasteful."
